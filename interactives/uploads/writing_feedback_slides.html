Strengthening Your Writing

Five common issues from your papers — and concrete strategies to fix each one.

1

Dropped Quotes

A quote appears in your paragraph without any introduction before it or analysis after it. The reader doesn't know why the quote is there or what it means for your argument.

Before
"Public transit ridership has increased by 34% in cities with dedicated infrastructure (Chen, 2021). Bikes are also a popular option in these areas."

The quote is dropped in with no setup, and the next sentence doesn't connect to it.
After
"A study from the University of Michigan found that investment in transit infrastructure directly drives ridership, reporting a 34% increase in cities that prioritized dedicated lanes (Chen, 2021). This suggests that supply of reliable transit, not just demand, is the key driver of behavioral change."

The quote is introduced, and the writer interprets what it means.
TIP

Introduce what the evidence is about, then follow up with your own interpretation of what it means for your argument.

2

Vague Language

Imprecise words like "good things," "situations," or "areas" don't communicate specific meaning. Your reader can't picture what you're describing.

Before
"People who live in areas with good things nearby tend to be happier and more productive in their daily situations."

What areas? What good things? What daily situations? The sentence says almost nothing.
After
"Residents with walkable access to grocery stores, parks, and transit stops report higher life satisfaction and lower stress levels."

Concrete nouns and measurable outcomes — the reader can see exactly what you mean.
TIP

Replace vague words with concrete, specific terms your reader can picture. If you can't name it precisely, you probably need to think more carefully about what you actually mean.

3

Citations Without Context

A source is cited for a claim it may not directly support, or an unexpected source is used without any explanation of why it's relevant.

Before
"Noise pollution has been shown to negatively affect student performance (Henderson et al., 2019)."

Henderson et al. is actually a study on hospital workflows — the reader has no idea why it's being cited here.
After
"Though primarily focused on workplace environments, Henderson et al.'s findings on noise disruption and cognitive performance have been widely applied to educational settings as well."

The writer explains the connection — now the reader trusts the citation.
TIP

Briefly explain the source's relevance so the reader understands why you chose it — especially when applying findings from a different context.

4

Underdeveloped Paragraphs

Some paragraphs make claims without specific evidence or analysis. They feel thin compared to the rest of the paper.

Before
"Another solution is green infrastructure. Cities that plant more trees and install green roofs see environmental benefits. This is important for fighting climate change going forward."

No source, no data, no analysis — just a vague claim.
After
"Green infrastructure offers measurable climate benefits at the city level. A 2022 EPA report found that urban tree canopy coverage reduces surface temperatures by up to 9°F in dense neighborhoods (EPA, 2022). This cooling effect directly reduces energy demand, lowering both costs and emissions for city residents."

Claim + source + specific evidence + analysis.
TIP

Every paragraph needs the same structure: a claim, a source, specific evidence, and your own analysis of what it means.

5

Conclusions That Lose Focus

The conclusion shifts tone, introduces new ideas, or drifts into motivational language instead of wrapping up the argument already made.

Before
"While congestion pricing may not be the perfect answer, it is a step in the right direction. Everyone should take time to learn about these issues and talk to their local representatives. Small actions add up and together we can build a better tomorrow."

This reads like a public service announcement, not a paper conclusion.
After
"The evidence presented here suggests that congestion pricing, when paired with reliable public transit alternatives, reduces both traffic volume and emissions without disproportionately burdening lower-income commuters. Cities considering this policy should prioritize transit investment alongside pricing to ensure equitable outcomes."

Synthesizes the paper's evidence into a clear, specific takeaway.
TIP

Synthesize your strongest evidence and state a clear, specific takeaway. No new arguments, no calls to action, no motivational language.

Quick Reference

Keep these five fixes in mind as you revise.

1

Dropped Quotes

Always introduce evidence and follow it with your interpretation.

2

Vague Language

Replace fuzzy words with concrete, specific terms.

3

Citations Without Context

Explain why a source is relevant, especially across fields.

4

Underdeveloped Paragraphs

Every paragraph: claim + source + evidence + analysis.

5

Conclusions That Lose Focus

Synthesize your evidence into a clear takeaway — no new arguments or motivational language.

These are the most common patterns across your papers.
Addressing even one or two of these will noticeably strengthen your writing.
